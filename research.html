Research

Optics is a core technology governing how humans and computers interact. From the LiDAR systems in autonomous vehicles to the displays in spatial computing, light is the primary carrier of information in the modern era. However, traditional optical hardware—relying on bulky, inefficient refractive elements—has become a critical bottleneck for emerging applications.

Our lab aims to create next-generation optical human-computer interfaces by integrating three synergistic domains: Nanophotonics, Optical System Engineering, and Physical Artificial Intelligence (AI). We are fundamentally reshaping optical hardware to create new ways to perceive, interact with, and compute digital information.

Nanophotonic Devices

Conventional lenses and prisms are constrained by the natural properties of their materials, limiting device functionality and compactness. We break these limits using nanophotonics. By engineering artificial structures at the subwavelength scale, we create ultrathin metamaterials and metasurfaces that allow precise control of the wavefront, intensity, polarization, and spectrum of light.

Our work has introduced unprecedented degrees of freedom in optical design, such as achieving the complete, simultaneous control of amplitude and phase in a single nanoscale device (Lee et al., Nanoscale 2018). Backed by extensive expertise in nanofabrication and novel material recipes, we ensure our theoretical designs translate into experimentally robust physical devices.

Research Directions

Our research interests span a broad range of application domains where light carries and shapes information. In the near term, we are especially focused on two directions.

Humans ↔ Computers

Photonic Interfaces for Human–Computer Interaction

How can light make digital information feel naturally embedded in the world around us? Today's near-eye displays still face a difficult trade-off: immersive 3D visual experiences require sophisticated optical control, while lightweight glasses-like form factors leave little room for bulky lenses, waveguides, and projection systems.

We develop compact optical systems for augmented and virtual reality by combining nanophotonic devices, holographic displays, and AI-driven computational imaging. Instead of treating optical hardware and algorithms as separate components, we co-design the full optical stack — from metasurface optics and waveguides to learned hologram generation and aberration correction.

This direction builds on previous work on metasurface eyepieces for augmented reality, neural holographic near-eye displays, and full-colour 3D holographic AR displays with metasurface waveguides. The goal is to create lightweight, immersive, and visually natural display systems that allow humans and computers to interact through light.

Computers ↔ Quantum Matter

Photonic Interfaces for Quantum Matter and Computing

How can optical hardware help computers control physical and quantum systems at scale? Neutral-atom quantum computers are a powerful example: atoms can serve as programmable quantum bits, but scaling this platform requires precise optical trapping, dynamic control, and high-fidelity readout of many atoms in parallel.

We develop nanophotonic and integrated optical systems that bring this control closer to the chip. By combining metasurfaces, photonic integrated circuits, and computational design, we aim to create compact optical platforms for trapping, arranging, addressing, and reading out neutral atoms. These systems could reduce the complexity of free-space optical setups and provide new routes toward scalable quantum processors.

This direction extends our photonic design philosophy into quantum technologies. Rather than studying quantum computation itself as an algorithmic problem, we focus on the optical interface that connects classical computers to quantum matter — the photonic hardware layer that allows information to be delivered to, manipulated within, and extracted from physical quantum systems.

Our Approach

Across both research directions, we use a full-stack photonic design philosophy. We engineer light from the nanoscale structure, to the optical system, to the computational algorithm that drives it. This approach combines three synergistic capabilities.

Nanophotonics

We design and fabricate subwavelength optical structures that control the amplitude, phase, polarization, spectrum, and spatial distribution of light. Metasurfaces and other nanophotonic devices allow us to replace bulky optical components with ultrathin, multifunctional elements tailored for specific system-level goals.

Optical Systems Engineering

We integrate nanophotonic devices into complete optical platforms, including near-eye displays, holographic imaging systems, and atom-control architectures. At this level, the goal is not only to improve an individual optical element, but also to rethink the entire optical architecture to achieve new combinations of compactness, efficiency, field of view, resolution, and scalability.

While nanophotonics offers incredible control at the device level, its true potential is realized only when integrated into functional systems. A major focus of our lab is overcoming the fundamental trade-offs between form factor and performance in modern wearable displays.

We pioneer the holistic integration of nanophotonics into full optical system prototypes. By reimagining optical architectures, we have developed see-through metasurface eyepieces that dramatically expand the field of view for immersive augmented reality (Lee et al., Nature Communications 2018). Furthermore, we recently demonstrated the first compact, optical see-through AR system capable of delivering true 3D holographic content in a wearable form factor using inverse-designed metasurface waveguides (Gopakumar and Lee et al., Nature 2024).

Computational Optics & Physical AI

Real-world optical systems inevitably face physical limitations, optical aberrations, and nanofabrication imperfections that prevent them from achieving theoretical perfection. To bridge the gap between simulation and reality, we integrate computational optics and Physical AI into the hardware loop.

By developing AI models grounded in physical principles and trained on actual experimental data, we can computationally predict, simulate, and invert complex optical processes. For instance, we use these models to computationally "pre-distort" input holograms, canceling out hardware errors and transforming a noisy prototype into a high-fidelity display. This hardware-software co-optimization extends across diverse platforms, including chip-scale quantitative phase imaging for biomedical microscopes.

Physics-Informed Machine Learning & Computational Optics

We use computational models and learning-based algorithms that are grounded in the physics of light propagation, light–matter interaction, and optical measurement. These tools allow us to inverse-design photonic hardware, compensate for aberrations and fabrication errors, and close the gap between ideal simulations and real experimental systems.
